Our Story

Born from Detroit's
Grit & Grace

Peirre Doresy didn't grow up with a film school education or industry connections. He grew up on the east side of Detroit with a camcorder, a library card, and an unshakable belief that the stories being lived on his block were worth more than anything Hollywood had produced in a decade.

In 2018, Dorsey Films was born in a converted warehouse in Midtown Detroit — part production studio, part talent incubator, part community sanctuary. What started as short-form docudramas capturing Detroit's post-industrial renaissance quickly evolved into a full-scale production company with international distribution deals and a talent pipeline that scouts from Detroit's high schools, community theaters, and neighborhood streets.

Our philosophy is simple: authentic storytelling requires authentic voices. We don't manufacture stars. We discover them, develop them, and give them the tools to illuminate the human experience on screen.

Peirre Doresy
Founder & Executive Producer

"The Last Shift"

Character: Marcus, 58 — Retired Auto Worker

You want to know what they never show you in the commercials? The last day. They don't show you the last day when you walk out of that plant and the door closes behind you and you realize — that sound. That sound you've been listening to for thirty-four years. You can't hear it anymore.

I built Chevys. I built Fords. I built pieces of this country's backbone while this country forgot my name. And when they finally gave me that watch — that watch with the fake gold that turns green before the year is out — I smiled. I shook the foreman's hand. I didn't cry until I got to my car.

My son thinks I should be relieved. Retired. He says that word like it's a gift. Maybe it is. Maybe I just don't know how to unwrap it yet.

"Letters to Nobody"

Character: Deja, 19 — First-Generation College Student

Every Sunday my grandmother writes me a letter. A real letter, on paper, in her handwriting that slants right like she's always leaning into the wind. She doesn't email. She doesn't text. She writes.

She tells me about the church and the neighbor's new car and the weather in Detroit like the weather in Detroit is something I need to know in Boston. And I keep every single one of them in a shoebox under my dorm room bed.

Because some nights — some nights when I feel like the most invisible person in every room I walk into — I open that box and I remember that somewhere, somebody knows where I come from. Somebody is writing my name down in ink that doesn't delete.

"Renaissance Man"

Character: Jerome, 42 — Self-Proclaimed Detroit Renaissance Man

People ask me what I do and I tell them: I'm a Renaissance Man. That means I do everything. I do a little DJ work on the weekends, I detail cars on Tuesdays and Thursdays, I'm currently in the process of writing a cookbook — it's mostly chili recipes — and I'm also developing a sneaker line.

My cousin says I need to "pick one thing and commit." She says that to me while working her one job that makes her miserable every single day. I respectfully decline her advice.

Detroit didn't get rebuilt by people who did one thing. Detroit got rebuilt by people who did everything, all at once, with whatever they had. The sneaker line is almost done, by the way. I just need a logo. And a manufacturer. And investors. But the name is solid — Phoenixs. With an 's.' It's plural because there's more than one phoenix in this city.

"Apology Tour"

Character: Renee, 35 — Woman Addressing Estranged Mother

I rehearsed this. I actually rehearsed this in the mirror for three days, which — I know — sounds like something a crazy person does. But I needed to be ready because every time I get in front of you, my voice just... disappears.

I'm not here to relitigate everything. I know you have your version and I have mine and God has the real version and we'll probably never fully agree. But I am here to say that I'm sorry for the years I spent punishing you for not being who I needed you to be. That wasn't fair.

[Beat.]

I brought you flowers. I know you don't like flowers in the house because of the pollen but I bought them anyway and now I'm standing here holding these flowers and I don't know what to do with them and I think that's a pretty accurate summary of our relationship, honestly.

"The Eulogy"

Character: Calvin, 50 — Speaking at His Father's Funeral

My father — and I want to say this with all the love in my heart — was the most stubborn man God ever put on this earth. This man refused to take directions. From anyone. Including MapQuest. Including actual paper maps. Including a compass one time in 1998 on a camping trip I will never fully recover from.

[Pause. Voice breaking slightly.]

But here's the thing about stubbornness when it's wrapped in love — it looks a lot like faith. He never doubted any of us. Not once. Not when I dropped out of college, not when my sister opened that restaurant that burned down — not one time did this man look at one of his children and say, "I'm not sure about you."

He was sure. He was always, always sure. And I didn't understand, until right now, standing here without him, how much of my own confidence was just his confidence living in me.

"Invisible Ink"

Character: Simone, 28 — Emerging Spoken Word Artist

They want you to be authentic. They want your rawness, your pain, your grandmother's recipes and your grandfather's arrest record and everything beautiful and broken about where you come from. They want all of that on stage, in a cup, for $14 with oat milk.

And then — when you give it to them, when you actually open your chest and let them see — they call it "powerful." They say "I had no idea." And you are proud. You are. But you're also standing there, a little emptier than you were when you walked in, wondering if maybe you should have kept something back.

My mother says: write it all down anyway. She says the things you keep inside rot. She says put it in ink, baby. Put it in ink so it outlasts us both.

So I write it all down. I just make sure I'm writing it for me first. And if you happen to see yourself in it — good. That means you're not as alone as you thought.

"The Interview"

Character: Brianna, 24 — First Real Job Interview

Okay. Okay. I have been preparing for this interview for two weeks. I have a folder on my desktop labeled "Career Goals" with seventeen documents in it. I have watched fourteen YouTube videos about "power poses." I am wearing a blazer I borrowed from my aunt who is four inches shorter than me and I have checked my teeth in every reflective surface in this building.

I am ready. They're going to ask me about my greatest weakness and I'm going to say "I work too hard" and they're going to see right through that but I'm going to say it with such confidence that they'll hire me anyway.

[Beat.]

My name is on the door. They just called my name. Lord, please let this blazer look intentional.

"Renovation Season"

Character: Yvonne, 45 — Homeowner in Gentrifying Neighborhood

The coffee shop opened in March. On the corner where Mr. Tatum used to fix cars out of his garage for thirty years, there is now a coffee shop with Edison bulbs and a chalkboard menu and a latte named "The Detroiter" that costs six dollars and contains no memories of Detroit whatsoever.

And the thing is — [sighs] — it's actually a really good latte. Which makes me furious. I wanted to hate it completely and I can't. I'm in there every Thursday with my oat milk cortado feeling like a traitor to my own ZIP code.

My daughter says: "Mom, you can appreciate a good coffee shop and also grieve the neighborhood you grew up in." She's twenty-two. She's annoyingly wise. I'm choosing to be irritated by her wisdom for at least another year.

"The River Between Us"

Character: Thomas, 16 — Young Man at the Detroit-Windsor Border

You can see Canada from here. Did you know that? From the Detroit Riverwalk you can literally see another country. Another country where people look just like us, talk just like us, bleed just like us — and have a completely different life just across the water.

My history teacher told us that Detroit was the last stop on the Underground Railroad. That freedom was right here, always visible, always close enough to swim to if you were brave enough. She said some people swam it. In November. In the dark.

[Long pause. Looking at the river.]

I'm going to college next year. First one in my family. My mom doesn't say much about it because she doesn't know how to talk about big things without crying. But last night she folded all my new college shirts. Folded every single one of them. And put them in a stack by the door. Like she's already sending me forward. Like she's already proud.

"Second Wind"

Character: Lydia, 62 — Woman Returning to College at 62

My classmates keep calling me "inspiring." Every time I pull out my notebook, somebody says, "You're so inspiring." I don't know how to tell them that I'm not trying to inspire anyone. I'm trying to get my English degree because I was supposed to get it forty years ago and life had other plans and I am not going to die without finishing what I started.

I raised four kids. I buried a husband. I survived a recession that took our house and a pandemic that took my best friend. I have been through enough to fill ten novels. And now, finally, I am going to learn how to write them properly.

The professor asked us to write about something we know better than anyone else. I filled eight pages. It turns out, when you finally look at your life, it's actually kind of extraordinary. I just couldn't see it while I was inside of it.
